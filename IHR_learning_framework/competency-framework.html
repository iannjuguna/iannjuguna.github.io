IHR Competency Framework
WHO and the IDEALS Institute at Johns Hopkins University (JHU) School of Education are collaborating on the creation of a learner-centered and outcomes-based competency model that empowers IHR Stakeholders with the knowledge, skills and behaviours required to implement the IHR.

The NFP Competency Framework will address the capacity needs of NFPs through a mechanism that enables them to identify training materials relevant to their skills and knowledge gaps, whilst recommending and availing learning activities for current and future NFPs’ personal development.

IHR Stakeholders Competency Framework
The process of developing competency frameworks includes a comprehensive review of all IHR competencies through interviews and focus groups with relevant stakeholders.

WHO plans to gather data through interviews of key IHR stakeholders to identify the key competencies needed by the different sectors of IHR stakeholders including NFPs.
National IHR Focal Points Competency Framework
The draft NFP Competency Framework (NFP CF) matrix contains 142 behavioral indicators for mandatory and supplementary NFP functions distributed across 19 domains and five competency groupings: leadership, management, coordination, communication, and technical knowledge. The matrix further categorizes behavioral indicators in two ways:
  • Based on competency groupings, domains and subdomains.

  • Based on the extent to which the behavioral indicators support the NFP mandatory functions (mandatory, additional optional, or not otherwise stated in the NFP guide).
The additional optional functions are prerequisites to fulfilling the mandatory functions, while functions that do not fall under either category likely reflect the evolving nature of the NFP through 12 years of IHR implementation, as new functions that emerged in response to changing needs.
Learning Pathways Certification and Credentials Model
The following steps need to be taken during development of learning pathways and credentail models.
  • Identify the workforce and the expected intended formal workforce credential.
  • Build on pre-existing workforce knowledge and skills background through guided learning opportunities, knowledge building activities, practice exercises and conversations, reflections and mentorship activities, which also allow individuals to incorporate their own experiences. A key element is to have individuals from the workforce directly support the building of the framework.
  • Demonstrate key actions, behaviors or competencies learned through the framework in combination with previous experiences. This involves the sequencing of learning experiences and rewarding the learners with merit achievements that lead to formal workforce credentials.
